// Art-world precarity
Black Swan responds to the economic instability of artists and cultural workers.
Its 2021 essay states that contemporary art institutions pushed risk onto individual artists and failed to provide basic economic security for most artists.
Berlin art context
The project emerges from Berlin’s art scene.
Penny Rafferty’s 2018 essay directly names Berlin’s gentrification, class bias, and the weakening of project-space culture as part of the project’s context.
Post-2008 crisis logic
Rafferty frames the art world as a system shaped by capitalism, crisis, precarity, burnout, and survival rather than common good.
This matters because Black Swan is not just “using blockchain.” It is asking whether art infrastructure itself can be reorganized.
DAO / blockchain discourse
Black Swan takes the DAO seriously but also critically.
Their 2021 essay says they used blockchain and DAOs as “tools for the imagination,” not necessarily as literal blockchain infrastructure.
This is important: the project is not techno-utopian. It uses blockchain thinking to test governance, but it also distrusts full automation.
2021 Web3 / art institution moment
The project appeared during a moment when blockchain, DAOs, NFTs, and alternative ownership models were entering art discourse.
Goethe frames the broader question as whether blockchain could reorganize the art world through transparency, resource-sharing, and equal access to information.
Audience
The audience is not the general public first.
The intended audience is artists, cultural workers, curators, technologists, ecologists, alternative art spaces, and institutions interested in redistributing resources.
LAS describes Black Swan as placing resources into the hands of users rather than art institutions.
